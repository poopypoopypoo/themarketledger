Price target: $30  ·  ~60% downside
Intel has run up dramatically on the promise of a foundry turnaround, and the market has rewarded a story that has not yet shown up in the numbers. The company is posting negative GAAP earnings, burning $4.5 billion in levered free cash flow, operating at 34.8% gross margins against an industry average well above 50%, and losing CPU market share to AMD for the fourth consecutive year. Q1 2026 results beat on revenue but missed on GAAP EPS at -$0.12 versus -$0.09 expected. The stock trades at over 100x forward earnings. That is a premium multiple for a business that is, by every current financial metric, not performing like a premium business. The turnaround may eventually come. But the stock is already priced as though it already has.

Short thesis
1
The foundry is burning cash, not generating it. Intel Foundry lost approximately $2.5 billion in Q4 2025 alone, with cumulative 2025 foundry operating losses around $10.3 billion. Nearly all of that revenue comes from internal orders — external customer wins are negligible. The entire bull case rests on Intel becoming a credible contract manufacturer for outside customers at scale. That has not happened. Q1 2026 foundry guidance called for double-digit sequential revenue growth, but gross margin guidance of roughly 34.5% shows the profitability problem is nowhere near resolved.
2
The core business is losing ground on both fronts. Intel's Client Computing Group, which is roughly 60% of total revenue, declined 7% year over year in Q4 2025 while AMD's client segment grew 34% in the same quarter. In Data Center, Intel faces intensifying competition from AMD EPYC and custom silicon from hyperscalers who are increasingly designing their own chips. Neither segment has a credible near-term reversal catalyst, and the combined revenue trajectory has been declining since 2021.
3
The valuation assumes a perfect outcome that has not been earned. At over 100x forward earnings for a company with negative GAAP EPS, negative free cash flow, and sub-35% gross margins, the stock is priced for flawless execution of a transformation that is still in its earliest stages. The Nvidia equity stake is financial in nature, not a manufacturing commitment. Nvidia has no incentive to pull workloads from TSMC until Intel 18A proves itself at scale, and that proof has not arrived. Any foundry guidance miss, and Q1 results suggest that risk remains real, will reprice this stock quickly.
Key catalysts for the short
Q1 2026 results (reported)
Revenue beat consensus but GAAP EPS missed at -$0.12. Earnings quality problem remains unresolved heading into Q2.
No major external foundry win
Failure to announce a significant outside customer by mid-2026 would directly confirm the bear thesis and likely trigger a re-rating.
AMD share data
AMD's upcoming earnings will show continued CPU share gains. Each quarter of data makes Intel's client recovery narrative harder to sustain.
Risks to the short
CHIPS Act backstop: Government subsidies provide a financial floor that could extend Intel's runway well beyond what the fundamentals alone would support, delaying the re-rating thesis by a year or more.
Nvidia equity signal: The market has read Nvidia's equity stake as a vote of confidence in Intel's foundry capabilities. If Nvidia publicly commits manufacturing volume to Intel 18A, the multiple will expand significantly before it contracts.
18A yield breakthrough: A credible announcement of improved 18A yields alongside a major external customer win would be the single event most likely to break the short thesis entirely. This remains a low probability but non-trivial risk.
