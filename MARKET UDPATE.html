Weekly commentary

On Tuesday, April 15, the S&P 500 closed at 7,022.95, crossing 7,000 for the first time in its history. By Friday the index had extended further to 7,126. The move represents a full recovery from the Iran war-driven selloff that pushed the index to its March lows, completed in just 11 trading sessions.

Markets absorbed considerable geopolitical stress to open the week. A failed U.S.-Iran ceasefire and a naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz briefly pushed oil above $100 a barrel. By Friday, Iran announced the strait was reopening to non-Iranian commercial vessels, crude fell sharply, and the index closed at a new record. The resilience in the face of that headline risk reflects genuine underlying demand from institutional buyers.

Technology led the advance. The Nasdaq gained 7% on the week, with ServiceNow and Datadog each surging over 12%. Nvidia extended its run to 11 consecutive positive sessions, a record streak. The rotation back into growth from the value-led start to the year is now nearly complete, with growth outperforming value by over 11% from the March bottom.

Key stories
Historic milestone
The S&P 500 closed above 7,000 for the first time in its history on April 15, completing a V-shaped recovery from the March lows in just 11 sessions.
Tech leadership
Nasdaq gained 7% on the week. ServiceNow and Datadog each surged over 12%. Nvidia extended its winning streak to 11 consecutive sessions, its longest ever recorded.
Geopolitical
Iran reopened the Strait of Hormuz to non-Iranian commercial vessels on Friday, sending crude oil down 9.4% and removing the most significant macro overhang markets have faced in months.
Earnings
Bank of America, JPMorgan, Wells Fargo, Morgan Stanley, and Citigroup all reported Q1 beats. Analysts now project 18% full-year S&P 500 earnings growth for 2026, with the forward P/E sitting at 20.9x.
Watch
Netflix fell over 10% after Q2 guidance disappointed. Consumer confidence hit a record low per the University of Michigan survey, with low-income spending continuing to weaken despite the broader market strength.
Fed risk
President Trump stated he would remove Fed Chair Powell if he does not step aside when his term expires next month. Markets absorbed the comment without significant reaction, though any genuine threat to Fed independence warrants close attention.
